growing in, and growing out

—the lessons i’ve learned from putting my friends on a spreadsheet.

Can you count your friends on 10 fingers?

I thought I couldn't, until I did.

If you’re daring enough, I’d ask you to try as well. Take out a pen and paper and write it down, or maybe make a list in your head.

If you think in vectors, then congratulations: this task should be quite simple.

On the flipside, if you think in odd scribbles, then you might’ve sounded a little like me: “Do I include X? Wait, but I can’t forget about Y. But actually it’s been a while since we last talked, so maybe… not? No, that doesn’t feel right… are these ranked? Does Z still consider me a friend?” And blah blah blah. Off to nowhere-land.

So what do you do in that situation? Naturally, I put my friends on a spreadsheet.

(well—I lied. It’s more like multiple charts… don’t worry, I’ve redacted any names)

Exhibit 1: a table (updated quarterly) & the quadrant (one copy per quarter).

Exhibit 2: the whole spread, nice & censored.

Is that borderline insane? Absolutely. Truth be told, I would not recommend keeping a friend-tracker to anybody who has a tendency to spiral. Do I still keep track? No. I stopped at 2025’s Q2. But I learned much about friendships through doing so. Here’s the whole story:

Last fall, I realized I stopped seeing my closest friends in non-work-related contexts. Like myself, everyone was busy, overwhelmed, and had too many commitments to remember off the top of their heads.

I feel like there’s a name for this. Ah—yes! Adult friendships.

So here’s my brilliant solution. Back in first year, I participated in a psychology study that used charts to map the quality of friendships. These charts served as inspiration for creating my own: each one to be updated every quarter.

I thought if I wrote down the names of every single person I cared for, sorted them by the types of conversations we’d most often have (academic, career, life philosophy, etc.), and reflected on the health of the friendship, I would gain mental clarity and allocate enough time to the most important people in my life. So I did. And three things happened:

  1. I stayed close to people who cherished me back.
  • With much conviction, I told my Type-A friends whom I didn’t see naturally that we ought to block out an hour weekly/bi-weekly on our calendars to see each other. The habit only stuck when there was enthusiasm from both ends.
  • Effort is everything. I’m still tight with them now.
  1. I was forced to be intentional about which friendships I wanted to kindle.
  • Updating the charts each quarter was stressful. I would stare at names and struggle to add, remove, and shift them out of uncertainty and self-protective apathy.
  • Friendships are a vulnerable thing. I like to receive reassurance, so I started to give more of it.
  1. I didn’t care as much as I thought I did.
  • I had a close friend from whom I was slowly drifting. My mind convinced me that I was sad, but my body said otherwise. At heart, I felt rather neutral.
  • Follow the natural flow of friendships. You cannot force connections that are growing apart to grow together again.

My conclusions might appear obvious, but rarely do I hear them verbalized in conversations (thank you Isabel and Caroline for being firsts to bring them to light). As I reflected on the year and looked back at how the charts changed over time, there was nothing particularly new. These were patterns I experienced and unknowingly pondered throughout my whole life.

Of course, the reason why we rarely bring it up is because either we are afraid, or we are used to the change. But over the course of multiple friendships that have come, gone, latched on and ripped away, here are my takeaways:

  1. Maintaining deep connections will always require intention.
    We both need to reach out. Admit when we felt disconnected. Communicate. As our time becomes increasingly valuable, time spent on each other will require more thought than either of us are used to. On days where I’m tired, overwhelmed, or feel alone, I still need to remind myself that friendships take two (and I’m half of it).
    With those who matter, always strive to reschedule—not cancel.     Time and love, in their purest forms, are always free.
  1. You do not need to stay close friends out of loyalty to the time you've spent together or the important conversations you’ve had.
    My relationship with someone is not with one person, but with every version of them I’ve met. And yes, they are all different and ever-changing, as am I. And if one day, I meet a version of them that no longer clicks with, relates to, or inspires the current version of me, I can—and sometimes should—take a few steps back.
    Grip on tightly and let go softly.
  1. You don’t care as much as you think you do. Nobody cares as much as they think they do.
    When I’m the first to realize that a connection is fading, I’ll grieve what once was, but my body, by that point, won’t feel so bad at all. When I’m the last to realize, I’ll close my eyes, turn my attention to my shoulders, lungs, and heels. Perhaps they’re not echoing the same sentiments as my heart… (I think she’s still remembering the past rather than receiving the present).
    Old friendships must leave the train for new ones to step on.

I have a tendency to change/grow frequently. The unique challenge that it presents is that my friendships have evolved and dissolved equally as frequently. I don’t mean to sound like a psycho… only a few people know me as one.

While I’ve long gotten used to it, a part of me still hopes it won’t forever be the case. After all, I’d like to be witnessed for a long time too.

Now, I could count my friends on ten fingers.

And for said friends who are reading, my decision to write and share a rather blunt perspective on friendship is not a promise that our relationship is doomed—that it will someday meet its end. In fact I truly hope they don’t. I have never ached for a friendship to die.

I hope we’ll continue to grow and inspire and console each other as we often do. I hope we’ll make each other laugh at our weddings and baby showers, hear about each others’ feats and falls, crash on each others’ couches, and watch our failure to apply sunscreen take revenge on our skin. I hope we’ll someday have conversations that remind us we were once young together.

So let me ask again.

Can you count your friends on 10 fingers?
